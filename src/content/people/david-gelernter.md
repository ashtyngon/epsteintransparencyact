David Gelernter is a computer scientist and a professor at Yale University. He is recognized for pioneering contributions to parallel computation in the 1980s, including the Linda coordination system developed with Nicholas Carriero, and for his 1991 book *Mirror Worlds*, which has been described as anticipating aspects of the World Wide Web.

Gelernter corresponded with Jeffrey Epstein from 2009 to 2015. Reporting by The Nation, drawing on records released under the Epstein Files Transparency Act, dates the exchanges to that period and describes individual messages — including an October 2011 email in which Gelernter recommended a Yale undergraduate for a position, using language that became the focus of subsequent coverage. The Nation reports the email was sent after Epstein's 2008 Florida conviction.

According to Newsweek, Yale suspended Gelernter from teaching pending a conduct review, and the university stated that it "does not condone the action taken by the professor or his described manner of providing recommendations for his students." Newsweek and The Nation both report that Gelernter defended himself: Newsweek quotes him saying "I'm very glad I wrote the note" and that he "did nothing that dishonored her in any conceivable way," and The Nation reports he characterized the disclosure as a violation of privacy and said he was unaware of Epstein's criminal history at the relevant time. The Nation notes Gelernter announced to his students on February 10, 2026 that he had been relieved of teaching his class.
