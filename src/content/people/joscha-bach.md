Joscha Bach is a German cognitive scientist and artificial-intelligence researcher, born in 1973, whose work focuses on cognitive architectures, mental representation, and the philosophy of mind, according to his Wikipedia biography. He earned an MA in computer science from Humboldt University of Berlin and a PhD in cognitive science from Osnabrück University, and his Wikipedia entry lists affiliations including the MIT Media Lab, Harvard's Program for Evolutionary Dynamics, Intel, and the AI Foundation, where he served as vice president of research.

Bach appears on Wikipedia's "List of people named in the Epstein files," which describes him as "a cognitive scientist whose MIT Media Lab research has been subsidized by Epstein." According to The Boston Globe, Epstein donated a total of $300,000 to support Bach's research at the MIT Media Lab across three contributions in November 2013 and July and September 2014, and the report states Bach was hired "in large part because Epstein subsidized the cost." Epstein's 2008 conviction predated this funding. The Globe reported that an email chain between Bach and Epstein from 2016, released by the U.S. House Oversight Committee in November 2025, contained remarks by Bach about race and gender and a statement describing fascism as "probably the most efficient and rationally stringent way of governance." The Globe reported that the AI company Liquid AI, which Bach had advised, terminated its relationship with him after the emails became public, and that MIT declined to comment specifically.

In his own public statement, "On the Jeffrey Epstein Affair," Bach wrote that respected scientists had recommended the collaboration and that he was assured Epstein was reformed, adding, "I have not met a single person from his network of academics who was aware of any instance of him breaking the law after his conviction." He said Epstein "never influenced the topics or goals of my research." Bach did not retract the 2016 emails but reframed them as a private scientific discussion, writing, "I do not believe (and have never said) that people of different races have different cognitive abilities." The Boston Globe likewise reported that Bach said his views had changed and that "race is NOT causal for differences in development."
