**Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick** is scheduled to testify about a 2012 visit to Jeffrey Epstein's private island, according to Arise News.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The testimony will address Lutnick's presence at **Little St. James**, Epstein's private Caribbean island.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The 2012 visit occurred three years after Epstein's 2008 conviction on state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida and his subsequent 13-month jail sentence.

Lutnick's connection to Epstein has [become a political liability for congressional Republicans](/news/2026-02-15-commerce-secretary-lutnick-s-epstein-ties-become-gop-liability) as documents revealing ties between the Commerce Secretary and the convicted sex offender have emerged in recent weeks.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Epstein's Island Operations

Little St. James served as Epstein's primary residence for decades before his 2019 arrest on federal sex trafficking charges. Court documents and victim testimony revealed that the island was a central location where Epstein allegedly abused underage girls, with some victims describing being trafficked there during the late 1990s and 2000s.

By 2012, Epstein had already served his controversial jail sentence under a non-prosecution agreement that has since been widely criticized. Despite his conviction, Epstein continued to maintain relationships with prominent figures in business, politics, and academia.

Epstein died in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal charges. In 2021, his estate sold both Little St. James and the adjacent property, Great St. James, to a private buyer for $60 million.

## Congressional Scrutiny

The scheduled testimony comes as multiple congressional committees have increased their focus on individuals who maintained contact with Epstein after his 2008 conviction. Recent weeks have seen several high-profile figures called to provide sworn testimony about their interactions with the convicted sex offender.

The [Epstein Transparency Act](/news/epstein-transparency-act-signed-into-law), signed into law in December 2024, requires federal agencies to declassify and release documents related to Epstein's activities and associates. The law has prompted renewed congressional interest in examining the full scope of Epstein's network and the extent of his continued social and business connections following his conviction.

The Commerce Department has not released a statement regarding Lutnick's scheduled testimony.
