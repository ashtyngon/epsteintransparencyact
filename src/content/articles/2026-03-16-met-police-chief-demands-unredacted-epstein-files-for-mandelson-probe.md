Britain's most senior police official plans to travel to the United States to request unredacted versions of FBI documents concerning **Peter Mandelson**, according to reports.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup><sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The Metropolitan Police Commissioner is seeking access to files released under the Epstein Transparency Act that contain references to the British politician and current ambassador to Washington.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The push for full document access comes as British authorities examine materials that surfaced following recent U.S. government disclosures.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Mandelson's name appears in FBI files released in recent weeks, prompting questions about the scope of his connections to Jeffrey Epstein's network.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

## Metropolitan Police Seeks Full Files

The Met chief is pressing U.S. authorities to provide complete, unredacted versions of documents that have been released to the public with portions blacked out.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The visit represents an effort to obtain investigative materials that may contain details not visible in publicly available versions.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

British law enforcement's interest in the files follows the January 2025 enactment of legislation requiring federal agencies to declassify and release Epstein-related records.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The FBI began releasing documents pursuant to that law in recent weeks, with some files containing references to foreign nationals including British citizens.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Diplomatic Complications

Mandelson currently serves as Britain's ambassador to the United States, a position he assumed in 2025.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> His dual role as both a subject of the records request and a senior diplomatic official presents unusual circumstances for the cross-border inquiry.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The Metropolitan Police has not publicly disclosed the nature of its investigation or whether Mandelson is being examined as a witness, subject, or in another capacity.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> British authorities have not announced whether the request for unredacted files relates to a formal criminal investigation.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The FBI has released thousands of pages of Epstein-related documents since the transparency law took effect, though many contain redactions citing privacy concerns, ongoing investigations, or national security considerations.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> U.S. officials have not indicated whether they will grant foreign law enforcement access to unredacted versions of files that remain partially sealed for American requesters.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>
