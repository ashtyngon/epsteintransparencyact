Epstein survivor **Jena-Lisa Jones** expanded her public advocacy campaign in late March, meeting with a member of Congress on Capitol Hill and accepting a keynote speaking role at one of the country's longest-running anti-sexual-violence events, according to multiple reports.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Jones, who has become one of the most visible survivors of **Jeffrey Epstein**'s sex trafficking operation, met with Rep. **Sydney Kamlager-Dove** (D-CA) to press for the full release of the Department of Justice's investigative file on Epstein and his associates.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The meeting was part of Jones's ongoing effort to engage lawmakers from both parties in the push for complete transparency under the Epstein Files Transparency Act, which Congress passed and President Trump signed into law earlier this year.

## Capitol Hill Advocacy

Jones told the Detroit News that she traveled to Washington specifically to make the case to individual members of Congress that the DOJ's document releases remain incomplete.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> She has argued publicly that the department's pace of disclosure has been too slow and that significant redactions have obscured critical information about Epstein's network.

Kamlager-Dove, who represents California's 37th Congressional District and serves on the House Foreign Affairs Committee, met with Jones to discuss the status of the file releases and potential legislative mechanisms to compel fuller disclosure.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Jones has spoken to media outlets across the country in recent months, detailing her own experience as a victim of Epstein's trafficking network and calling on the federal government to prioritize survivor access to documents related to their cases.

Her advocacy has focused on the distinction between the files released to the public and the complete investigative file that the DOJ maintains internally. Survivors and their legal representatives have argued that the full file contains information relevant to ongoing civil litigation and potential future criminal proceedings.

## Take Back the Night Keynote

The Michigan Daily reported that Jones was announced as the keynote speaker for the 48th Annual Take Back the Night rally in Ann Arbor, scheduled for April 2.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The event, organized by the University of Michigan's Sexual Assault Prevention and Awareness Center, is one of the oldest continuously running Take Back the Night events in the United States.

WNEM reported that the invitation reflected Jones's growing profile as a survivor-advocate and her willingness to share her story in public forums to advance accountability for Epstein's network.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

Take Back the Night events, which originated in the 1970s, are held annually at universities and communities across the country to raise awareness about sexual violence and support survivors. The selection of an Epstein survivor as keynote speaker connects the broader anti-sexual-violence movement to the specific accountability campaign surrounding the Epstein files.

## A Growing Public Voice

Jones's dual role as a Capitol Hill advocate and public speaker represents a shift in the Epstein accountability movement, which has increasingly been led by survivors rather than attorneys or journalists. Her willingness to identify herself publicly and describe her experiences in detail has drawn both support and scrutiny.

Victim advocacy groups have praised Jones's efforts, noting that survivor voices have historically been marginalized in discussions about how institutions should respond to trafficking networks. Jones has emphasized that the fight for document transparency is not an abstract policy debate but a matter directly affecting the lives of women who were victimized as minors.

Jones told reporters that she plans to continue meeting with lawmakers and speaking publicly until the DOJ releases the complete investigative file without redactions that she believes obscure the identities of individuals who enabled Epstein's activities.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The Department of Justice has not commented on Jones's specific requests for additional disclosures beyond the releases already made under the transparency act.
