President **Donald Trump** fired Attorney General **Pam Bondi** on April 2, multiple outlets reported, amid growing frustration over her handling of the Department of Justice's release of documents related to **Jeffrey Epstein**.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Bondi had served as the nation's top law enforcement officer during the implementation of the Epstein Files Transparency Act, the landmark legislation Trump signed earlier this year requiring federal agencies to declassify and publish records tied to Epstein's activities and associates.

## Bipartisan Criticism Preceded Firing

Bondi drew fire from both sides of the aisle over the DOJ's approach to the file releases. Democrats accused her office of selectively redacting documents to shield politically connected individuals, while Republicans expressed frustration with the pace of compliance.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The tension escalated in March when the House Oversight Committee issued a bipartisan subpoena compelling Bondi to testify under oath about the DOJ's review process. Five Republican members joined all Democrats in authorizing the subpoena, a rare cross-party rebuke of a sitting attorney general from the president's own party.

Multiple White House sources told CNN that Trump had grown increasingly dissatisfied with the public perception that his administration was not fully committed to transparency on the Epstein matter.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup> The president had repeatedly touted the transparency act as a signature achievement and was frustrated that the DOJ's handling of releases was generating negative headlines.

## Blanche Expected to Serve as Interim AG

Deputy Attorney General **Todd Blanche**, who previously served as one of Trump's personal defense attorneys before joining the administration, is expected to assume the role of acting attorney general, according to NBC News.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

Blanche was present alongside Bondi during the contentious closed-door briefing on March 18 when Democratic members of the House Oversight Committee walked out in protest. His elevation to the top DOJ post could reshape the department's approach to the remaining Epstein document releases.

The firing marks the first time a Trump-era attorney general has been removed over the Epstein files specifically, though the issue has shadowed multiple administrations. Legal analysts noted that the move could signal a shift in the pace and scope of future releases.

White House press secretary did not immediately respond to requests for comment. The DOJ issued a brief statement acknowledging Bondi's departure without providing additional details about the circumstances.

Advocacy groups that have pushed for full disclosure of Epstein-related records offered mixed reactions. Some praised the move as a sign that Trump was serious about transparency, while others cautioned that replacing the attorney general would not address systemic issues within the DOJ's review process.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

The DOJ has published approximately 3.5 million pages under the transparency act, though lawmakers from both parties have disputed whether that figure represents full compliance. An estimated 2.5 million additional documents from investigative files remain unreleased.
