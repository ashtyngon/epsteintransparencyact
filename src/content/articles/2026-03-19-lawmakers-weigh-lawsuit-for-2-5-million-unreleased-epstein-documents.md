A CNN investigation has determined that approximately 2.5 million Department of Justice documents related to **Jeffrey Epstein** remain unreleased, even as lawmakers on both sides of the aisle weigh legal action to force their disclosure.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The finding comes after the DOJ published roughly 3.5 million pages of Epstein-related records under the Epstein Files Transparency Act. However, CNN's review found that many of those released pages are so heavily redacted that they provide little meaningful information about Epstein's network of associates or the scope of his criminal operations.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Bipartisan Push for Legal Action

Rep. **Thomas Massie** (R-KY) and Rep. **Ro Khanna** (D-CA) told CNN they are considering joining Epstein survivors in filing a lawsuit to compel the release of the remaining documents.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The bipartisan pairing underscores the frustration that has built across party lines over the DOJ's handling of the transparency mandate.

"The American people were promised transparency," Massie said. <sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The potential lawsuit gained legal footing after Judge **Paul Engelmayer** ruled that the criminal case against **Ghislaine Maxwell** was not the appropriate venue for challenging the DOJ's document withholdings.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> However, Engelmayer's ruling explicitly left open the possibility that interested parties could sue in their personal capacities through a separate civil action.

## Scale of Withheld Records

The 2.5 million unreleased documents reportedly include FBI investigative files, grand jury materials, and internal DOJ communications about the Epstein case. Legal experts told CNN that some of the withheld records may be subject to legitimate exemptions, including grand jury secrecy rules and ongoing investigation protections.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

But survivors and their advocates argue that the DOJ has applied those exemptions too broadly, shielding records that could reveal the identities of co-conspirators who have never been charged. The Epstein Files Transparency Act was designed to override many traditional exemptions, but the executive branch retains discretion over national security and law enforcement classifications.

The sheer volume of withheld material has become a political flashpoint. Advocacy groups have pointed out that the 2.5 million unreleased pages dwarf the 3.5 million that have been made public, suggesting the majority of the government's Epstein-related records remain hidden from public view.

Massie and Khanna have not set a timeline for filing a lawsuit but said they are in discussions with attorneys representing Epstein survivors about the legal strategy.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>
