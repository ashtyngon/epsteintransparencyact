House Oversight Committee Chairman **James Comer** (R-KY) formally issued a subpoena to Attorney General **Pam Bondi** compelling her to appear for a deposition regarding what the committee described as "possible mismanagement" of the **Jeffrey Epstein**-Maxwell investigation, the Washington Post reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The subpoena sets an April 14 date for the deposition. It was authorized by a bipartisan committee vote of 24-19 on March 4, with five Republicans crossing party lines to join all Democrats in approving the measure.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Bipartisan Authorization

The five Republican votes in favor of the subpoena included Rep. **Nancy Mace** (R-SC), who has been among the most vocal GOP critics of DOJ handling of Epstein files. Rep. Thomas Massie (R-KY), a co-author of the Epstein Transparency Act, also voted to authorize the subpoena.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The bipartisan margin was notable in a committee where partisan divides have characterized most oversight activity during the current Congress. Fox News reported that the five Republican defections caught White House officials off guard, as administration allies on the committee had been expected to block the subpoena.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

Rep. Ro Khanna (D-CA), who has worked with Mace and Massie on Epstein transparency legislation, praised the bipartisan vote. Khanna said it demonstrated that "accountability for the Epstein matter crosses party lines."

## Scope of Deposition

The subpoena requires Bondi to answer questions about several areas of DOJ activity related to Epstein files. These include the department's decisions about which documents to release and which to withhold, the extent and justification for redactions in published documents, and communications between DOJ and the White House about the file releases.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Committee staff said the deposition would also cover Bondi's public claim that all Epstein files had been released, which lawmakers from both parties have disputed. The committee has cited a CNN investigation finding that 2.5 million pages remain unreleased.

UPI reported that the subpoena also covers Bondi's knowledge of the FBI's Epstein holdings and any instructions she may have given regarding the pace or scope of releases.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

## DOJ Response

The Justice Department issued a statement saying it was "reviewing the subpoena and will respond through appropriate legal channels." The department did not indicate whether Bondi would comply.

DOJ attorneys have previously argued that compelling testimony from a sitting attorney general about internal deliberations raises executive privilege concerns. Legal experts said a court fight over the subpoena was possible but noted that the bipartisan vote strengthened the committee's legal position.

Bondi did not make a public statement in response to the subpoena. Her office referred inquiries to DOJ's congressional affairs unit.

## Mace's Role

Mace has been a persistent critic of DOJ handling of Epstein files since the initial document releases in February. She accused the department of concealing names through excessive redactions. Her vote to subpoena Bondi represented an escalation of that criticism.

Mace told reporters after the vote that "the American people deserve to know why the Justice Department is hiding information about Epstein's network." Her office said she had submitted questions focused on redaction decisions.

## Political Context

The subpoena came amid broader tensions between Congress and the Trump administration over Epstein-related transparency. The bipartisan nature of the vote increased pressure on the administration to cooperate, as previous partisan oversight disputes have often stalled when the majority party declined to enforce subpoenas against same-party officials.
