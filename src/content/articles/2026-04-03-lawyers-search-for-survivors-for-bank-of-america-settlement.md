Attorneys representing **Jeffrey Epstein** survivors have launched a search for victims eligible to receive compensation from Bank of America's $72.5 million settlement, Al Jazeera reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The settlement makes Bank of America the third major financial institution to resolve claims that it facilitated Epstein's criminal activities through its banking services. JPMorgan Chase previously settled for $290 million, and Deutsche Bank agreed to pay $75 million.

## Settlement Details

The $72.5 million agreement resolves allegations that Bank of America provided banking services to Epstein despite knowledge of suspicious transactions connected to his trafficking activities. Plaintiffs alleged the bank processed payments that facilitated the recruitment and transport of victims.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

A federal court hearing was held in April to review the terms of the proposed settlement. Judge **Jed Rakoff**, who is overseeing the case in the Southern District of New York, scheduled a final fairness hearing for August 27 to determine whether to grant final approval.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

Bank of America did not admit wrongdoing as part of the settlement. The bank said in a statement that it chose to resolve the matter to avoid the cost and distraction of prolonged litigation.

## Search for Eligible Survivors

Attorneys are now working to identify and locate all survivors who may be eligible for a portion of the settlement fund. The search involves outreach through survivor advocacy organizations, legal aid networks, and public notices in jurisdictions where Epstein was known to have operated.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Eligible claimants are expected to include individuals who were trafficked or abused by Epstein or his associates during the period when Bank of America maintained banking relationships that plaintiffs allege facilitated the criminal conduct.

A court-appointed claims administrator will review submissions and determine individual award amounts. Attorneys said some eligible survivors may not be aware of the settlement. The outreach effort is designed to reach victims in multiple states and countries, reflecting the geographic scope of Epstein's activities.

## Pattern of Financial Institution Settlements

The Bank of America settlement follows a pattern established by earlier cases against financial institutions that banked Epstein. The JPMorgan settlement of $290 million in 2023 was the largest, resolving claims that the bank maintained Epstein as a client despite internal warnings about his activities.

Deutsche Bank settled for $75 million in 2023 over similar allegations. That bank had taken Epstein as a client after JPMorgan ended its relationship with him.

Together, the three settlements total $437.5 million in compensation for Epstein survivors from financial institutions alone. Additional civil cases against other entities remain pending in multiple courts.

Legal analysts told Al Jazeera that these settlements represent the largest pool of compensation available to Epstein survivors.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Judge Rakoff's Oversight

Judge Rakoff, who has overseen several Epstein-related civil cases in the Southern District of New York, will determine whether the settlement terms are fair to the class of survivors. The August 27 hearing will allow class members to object to the terms or the distribution formula.

Rakoff allowed the underlying lawsuit to proceed last year after ruling that plaintiffs had stated viable claims against the bank. That decision put pressure on Bank of America to negotiate rather than face trial.

## Broader Accountability

Survivor advocates have cited the financial institution settlements as evidence that entities beyond Epstein bear responsibility for the harm caused by his criminal enterprise. NBC News reported that attorneys are reviewing potential claims against other financial institutions and service providers.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>
