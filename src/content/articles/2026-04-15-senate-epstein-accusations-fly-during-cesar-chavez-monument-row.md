A Senate floor dispute over defunding the César Chávez National Monument devolved into a public exchange of **Jeffrey Epstein**-related accusations between Democratic and Republican senators on April 15, illustrating how the Epstein scandal has become an all-purpose political weapon on Capitol Hill, CNBC reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The confrontation began when Sen. **Martin Heinrich** (D-NM) responded to Republican efforts to defund the monument by posting news headlines on social media showing that Sens. **Mike Lee** (R-UT) and **John Cornyn** (R-TX) had voted against legislation requiring the release of Epstein files.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## The Exchange

Heinrich's post drew a direct connection between the monument debate and the Epstein transparency issue, implying that Lee and Cornyn had something to hide by opposing the file releases.

Lee responded within hours by posting a 2012 email, obtained from the released Epstein files, in which Epstein asked an associate to arrange a lunch meeting with Heinrich. Lee captioned the post with a suggestion that Heinrich should explain his own Epstein connections before casting aspersions on others.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Heinrich denied ever meeting Epstein and said he had no knowledge of the email before it was made public through the document releases.

"I never met Jeffrey Epstein," Heinrich said in a statement. "I never had lunch with him. I never communicated with him. The fact that a convicted sex trafficker asked someone to set up a meeting does not mean that meeting ever took place."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Context of the Dispute

The underlying Senate debate concerned a Republican-backed measure to strip federal funding and national monument designation from the César Chávez National Monument in Keene, California. The monument honors the labor leader and civil rights activist and was designated by President Obama in 2012.

Heinrich, who represents New Mexico and has been a defender of public lands designations, was among the most vocal opponents of the defunding effort. His decision to bring Epstein into the debate represented an escalation of the rhetorical tactics both parties have employed around the scandal.

The exchange underscored the degree to which the Epstein files have permeated virtually every area of congressional activity, with lawmakers on both sides using the documents as political ammunition in disputes that have no direct connection to the case.

## Broader Pattern

Political analysts noted that the Epstein issue has become one of the most potent tools in the congressional arsenal, rivaling more traditional political attacks in its ability to generate attention and put opponents on the defensive.

"Every senator's name is potentially in those files somewhere," one congressional aide told CNBC. "It has become a game of mutually assured destruction."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The exchange also highlighted the challenges posed by the sheer volume of the released documents, which contain thousands of names, many of whom had only peripheral or nonexistent contact with Epstein. An email requesting a meeting does not establish that a meeting occurred, but the mere appearance of a senator's name in the files has proven sufficient to generate political attacks.

## No Resolution

Neither side backed down after the exchange. Lee did not retract or clarify his post, and Heinrich's office maintained his denial of any contact with Epstein.

The César Chávez monument defunding measure remained under debate on the Senate floor. Multiple observers noted that the Epstein-related exchange had overshadowed the actual policy discussion and generated far more media coverage than the monument issue itself.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>
