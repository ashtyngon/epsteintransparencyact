The Department of Justice published more than 1,000 new pages of **Jeffrey Epstein**-related files on March 5, including 16 previously withheld pages containing three FBI interview summaries from a woman who alleged that **Donald Trump** sexually abused her as a minor in the 1980s through an Epstein connection, NPR reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The department said the documents had been "incorrectly deemed duplicative" during earlier rounds of disclosure under the Epstein Files Transparency Act and should have been included in previous releases.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The release also restored approximately 50,000 files that had been previously removed from the public-facing database, according to reporting by the Washington Post.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The scope of the restoration raised questions among journalists and lawmakers about the thoroughness of the department's document review processes.

## The Withheld Interview Summaries

The 16 pages at the center of the disclosure contained three separate 2019 FBI interview summaries with a woman whose identity was redacted in the released documents.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The interviews were conducted as part of the bureau's investigation into Epstein's sex trafficking network following his July 2019 arrest on federal charges in New York.

NPR reported that the woman alleged sexual abuse by Trump when she was a minor, describing encounters she said were facilitated through Epstein during the 1980s.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The allegations have not been substantiated through criminal charges or civil proceedings.

The department's characterization of the documents as "incorrectly deemed duplicative" drew scrutiny from congressional investigators who questioned how interview summaries containing presidential allegations could be mistakenly classified as duplicates of existing material.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Missing Pages

NPR reporter **Roger Sollenberger** found that 37 pages remained missing from the released files, including notes and internal communications that appeared in document logs but were not included in the production.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The missing materials included what the logs described as internal FBI correspondence related to the investigation.

Al Jazeera reported that the gaps in the release fueled criticism from transparency advocates and some members of Congress who have argued that the Justice Department has not fully complied with the disclosure requirements of the Epstein Files Transparency Act.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

Attorney General **Pam Bondi** has faced bipartisan congressional pressure over the pace and completeness of the Epstein file releases. The House Oversight Committee issued a subpoena in March requiring Bondi to provide sworn testimony on the department's handling of the disclosures.

## The 2006 Case File

Among the newly released materials was what appeared to be the complete 2006 case file on the original Epstein-**Ghislaine Maxwell** investigation conducted by the FBI's Miami field office and the Palm Beach County State Attorney's office.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

PBS reported that the case file included investigative reports, witness statements, and internal assessments from the period before Epstein negotiated the controversial 2007 non-prosecution agreement with federal prosecutors in the Southern District of Florida.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

The 2006 case file is significant because it documents the early stages of law enforcement awareness of Epstein's activities and the decisions that led to his receiving what critics have called an unusually lenient plea deal. The non-prosecution agreement, negotiated by then-U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, allowed Epstein to plead guilty to state charges and serve 13 months in a county jail with work-release privileges.

## Congressional Response

The release prompted immediate reactions from lawmakers in both parties. Democratic members of the House Oversight Committee cited the previously withheld Trump-related documents as evidence that the department's review process required independent oversight.

Republican members focused on the scope of the restoration — the 50,000 files returned to the database — as a sign that the department was complying with its obligations under the transparency law, though they acknowledged that the initial removal of those files was troubling.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The White House did not issue a statement regarding the allegations contained in the newly released FBI interview summaries.
