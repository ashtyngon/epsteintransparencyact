Acting Attorney General **Todd Blanche** faces mounting obstacles to securing the permanent attorney general position, with his handling of **Jeffrey Epstein**-related matters emerging as a central vulnerability, NewsNation reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Blanche's July 2025 meeting with convicted sex trafficker **Ghislaine Maxwell** at a federal prison has drawn sustained scrutiny from lawmakers and advocacy groups. During that meeting, Blanche did not challenge Maxwell on her documented false statements about her role in Epstein's criminal enterprise, according to reports.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Prison Meeting Fallout

Maxwell was convicted in December 2021 on five federal charges, including sex trafficking of a minor. She is serving a 20-year sentence.

Following Blanche's prison visit, Maxwell was transferred from a low-security federal facility to a minimum-security prison, CNN reported.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The transfer raised questions among lawmakers about whether the meeting influenced her custody conditions.

DOJ officials have not publicly explained the rationale for Maxwell's facility transfer. Bureau of Prisons spokespeople declined to comment on individual inmate placement decisions when contacted by reporters.

Congressional Democrats have called for a full accounting of the circumstances surrounding both the meeting and the subsequent transfer. Republican members of the House Oversight Committee have also expressed interest in reviewing communications between Blanche's office and the Bureau of Prisons.

## 'Move On' Statements Compound Problem

Blanche's public declaration that the DOJ should "move on" from Epstein files added another complication to his potential confirmation prospects. The statement contradicted the bipartisan consensus in Congress that significant Epstein-related documents remain unreleased.

Senators from both parties have indicated that any confirmation hearing for a permanent attorney general would include extensive questioning about Epstein file releases and DOJ transparency. Blanche's stated position that the matter is closed could put him at odds with key votes needed for confirmation.

NewsNation reported that White House aides have privately acknowledged Blanche's Epstein-related controversies present a confirmation challenge.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Several Republican senators who supported the Epstein Transparency Act have not committed to supporting Blanche for the permanent role.

## Zeldin as Alternative

EPA Administrator **Lee Zeldin**, a former New York congressman, has emerged as a leading candidate to replace Bondi permanently, according to multiple reports.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Zeldin's name has circulated among White House advisers as an alternative who would face fewer confirmation hurdles related to the Epstein matter.

Zeldin served in the U.S. House from 2015 to 2023 and ran unsuccessfully for governor of New York in 2022. He has not been publicly linked to any Epstein-related controversies.

The White House has not formally announced a nominee for permanent attorney general. Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt said the president would make an announcement "when he's ready" and declined to comment on specific candidates.

## Confirmation Dynamics

A permanent attorney general nomination requires Senate confirmation by a simple majority. The current 53-47 Republican majority in the Senate leaves limited room for defections.

Senators who have publicly advocated for full Epstein file releases — including several Republicans — could pose difficulties for any nominee perceived as resistant to transparency. Blanche's combination of the Maxwell meeting and his public statements about moving past the files has created what one Senate aide described as a "two-front problem."

The Senate Judiciary Committee, which would hold confirmation hearings, includes members who have been outspoken about Epstein-related accountability. Committee leaders have not commented on potential timeline for any nomination.
