A CNN investigation published April 22, 2026 documented specific allegations from **Jeffrey Epstein** victims against two prominent figures — former Barclays CEO **Jes Staley** and Apollo Global Management co-founder **Leon Black** — in FBI interview memos released as part of the Epstein Files Transparency Act.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The CNN review found that more than a dozen victims told FBI agents during interviews that Epstein or **Ghislaine Maxwell** facilitated sexual encounters with other powerful men, naming more than half a dozen individuals. The allegations against Staley and Black are among the most specific identified by name in the review.

## Allegations Against Jes Staley

According to CNN's review of FBI interview memos:<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

- A victim said **Epstein directed her to massage Staley**.
- The victim alleged that Staley then **sexually assaulted her**.

Staley served as a senior executive at JPMorgan Chase from 2001 to 2013, during the period when JPMorgan maintained Epstein as a banking client despite internal red flags. He later became CEO of Barclays in 2015, a role he held until 2021 when he stepped down amid scrutiny of his ongoing communications with Epstein.

JPMorgan sued Staley in 2023, alleging he concealed the extent of his relationship with Epstein from the bank. The bank's lawsuit included email exchanges between the two men that prosecutors and journalists have characterized as deeply familiar. Staley has denied wrongdoing in connection with Epstein.

## Allegations Against Leon Black

CNN's review identified allegations from **multiple victims** against Black:<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

- One woman said Black **became "sexual" during a massage** that Epstein arranged for him.
- That woman said she **fled the room** during the encounter.
- Another woman alleged that Black **raped her in a separate incident** years earlier.

Black co-founded Apollo Global Management and served as its CEO until March 2021, when he stepped down following an outside review by the law firm Dechert. The review found Black had paid Epstein **$158 million** between 2012 and 2017 for what Black described as tax and estate planning advice.

The Dechert review made no allegations of wrongdoing against Black at the time. However, additional payments and concerns have continued to surface. Sen. **Ron Wyden** (D-Ore.), the ranking member of the Senate Finance Committee, set an April 13, 2026 deadline for Black to respond to questions about an additional **$12 million** in payments — bringing the total identified payments from Black to Epstein to roughly **$170 million**.

Wyden's investigation has cited evidence of potential hush money payments to women funneled through Epstein and possible money laundering concerns. Black has denied wrongdoing.

## What the Files Don't Show

The CNN investigation emphasized that the FBI memos:

- Generally **redacted the names** of accused men
- Did **not include corroborating investigative work**
- Did **not document** how federal agents followed up on the allegations
- Did **not record** any interviews with the named men

**FBI Director Kash Patel** has publicly stated there is "no credible information" that Epstein trafficked his victims to other men, despite the volume of victim accounts CNN reviewed.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

To date, only Epstein and Maxwell have been criminally charged in the United States with sex trafficking related to the case.

## Legal and Political Implications

The CNN reporting comes amid intensifying congressional scrutiny of how the Justice Department investigated Epstein's network. The DOJ Office of the Inspector General announced on April 23 it has opened a probe into the department's compliance with the Transparency Act, including how it handled redactions and follow-up investigations.

The named-by-victims allegations against Staley and Black do not constitute criminal charges, and neither man has been charged with wrongdoing in the Epstein case. The CNN review documents what victims told the FBI, not what evidence corroborates those accounts.
