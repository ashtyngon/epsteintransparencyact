House Oversight Committee Democrats released an interim staff report on May 12, 2026 arguing that the 2008 non-prosecution agreement that **Jeffrey Epstein** received from federal prosecutors did more than let him escape serious punishment — it gave him the freedom to build out an international trafficking network over the following years.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The report, titled "The Price of Non-Prosecution: The Evolution of Epstein's Trafficking Network, from Palm Beach to Paris and Beyond," was released alongside the Democrats' field hearing in West Palm Beach and uses new evidence obtained through committee subpoenas. According to MS NOW's account of the document, it concludes that the deal "permitted Epstein to develop an international trafficking operation" and allowed him to "continue and expand" his abuse.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup><sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

## What the 2008 Deal Did

The non-prosecution agreement was negotiated by **Alex Acosta**, then the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Florida. Under its terms, Epstein pleaded guilty in state court to a single count of solicitation of prostitution, served roughly 13 months in the Palm Beach County jail — much of it on work release — and registered as a sex offender. The federal government agreed not to prosecute him, and the deal also shielded potential co-conspirators.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The Democrats' report stresses how far the federal investigation had already advanced before that resolution. Investigators had assembled evidence from 34 victims and prepared a draft indictment, according to Latin Post's account of the report, when prosecutors instead agreed to the arrangement that ended the federal case.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Survivors have long called it a "sweetheart deal," and it was the same agreement that survivor **Courtney Wild** and others spent years challenging in federal court.

## "From Palm Beach to Paris"

The report's central argument is captured in its subtitle. Democrats say the years after 2008 — when Epstein was a registered sex offender but free — were when his operation went global, drawing women from abroad. **Rep. Robert Garcia**, the panel's ranking Democrat, said the records show Epstein exploited the visa system to bring women into the United States, part of what Democrats describe as the network's expansion beyond Florida.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

One survivor who testified at the accompanying hearing, a former model identified as **Roza**, said she was trafficked from Uzbekistan through a modeling pipeline tied to Epstein associate **Jean-Luc Brunel**, and that Epstein abused her even while he was under house arrest, according to Latin Post.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Brunel, a French modeling agent who was charged in France with rape of minors and human trafficking, died in a Paris jail cell in 2022.

## Political Frame

Garcia paired the report's historical findings with the Democrats' present-day complaint against the Trump administration, accusing the Justice Department of "breaking the law" by withholding Epstein records that Congress required it to disclose under the Epstein Files Transparency Act.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

As an interim report from the committee's minority, the document does not carry the force of an official committee finding, and it revisits an agreement that has been the subject of litigation and a 2020 Justice Department ethics review. That earlier review found Acosta used "poor judgment" but did not conclude he committed professional misconduct. What the new report adds is a through-line argument: that the cost of the 2008 leniency was measured in the additional victims harmed in the years that followed.

Acosta, who went on to serve as President Trump's Labor secretary before resigning in July 2019 amid renewed scrutiny of the Epstein deal, has defended his handling of the case, saying his office's intervention ensured Epstein served jail time and registered as a sex offender when the state had been prepared to let him avoid both.
