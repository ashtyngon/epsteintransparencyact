Survivors of **Jeffrey Epstein**'s sex trafficking operation and their legal representatives reacted to the firing of Attorney General **Pam Bondi** on April 3 with a mixture of frustration and skepticism, expressing little confidence that the leadership change would improve the Department of Justice's handling of Epstein-related matters.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Attorney **Gloria Allred**, who represents multiple Epstein survivors, called Bondi's departure "long overdue" in an interview with NewsNation.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

"Pam Bondi destroyed the trust in the DOJ," Allred said. "Survivors came forward, they cooperated, and they were met with obstruction, redaction failures, and indifference."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Survivors Speak Out

**Jess Michaels**, a survivor who has been vocal in calling for full transparency, told NewsNation that Bondi "failed us" during her tenure as attorney general.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

"She had the power to do the right thing, and she chose not to," Michaels said. However, Michaels said she held no optimism that acting Attorney General Todd Blanche would be any better. "We have no reason to believe anything is going to change."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

**Danielle Bensky**, another survivor who recently spoke publicly about having her identity exposed through DOJ document releases, told Fox Baltimore that she felt the department was "gaslighting" victims.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

"They tell us they are protecting us while they release our names," Bensky said. "They tell us they are pursuing justice while they close investigations. At some point, you have to call it what it is."<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

## Pattern of Broken Promises

Survivor advocates said the firing of Bondi, while notable, did not address the systemic problems with how the DOJ has handled the Epstein case over multiple administrations.

The Survivors of Epstein Network noted in a statement that survivors had been promised accountability by three consecutive attorneys general, none of whom had brought new charges against Epstein's known associates.

"The faces change, but the pattern stays the same," the statement read. "No new indictments. No meaningful accountability. Just more political theater."

Allred said the core problem was that the DOJ had treated the Epstein matter as a political issue rather than a law enforcement priority. She called on whoever became the permanent attorney general to appoint a special counsel with dedicated resources to pursue remaining investigative leads.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Concerns About Blanche

Several survivors and their attorneys expressed specific concerns about Blanche's appointment. His background as Trump's personal defense attorney raised questions about whether he could independently manage the Epstein files, given that some released documents contain references to the president.

"You cannot have the president's former personal lawyer deciding what Epstein files the public gets to see," said one attorney who represents three survivors and requested anonymity to protect client privacy.

Michaels said survivors were "exhausted" from the cycle of new leadership, new promises, and continued disappointment. "We are not going away," she said. "But we are running out of faith in this process."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The DOJ did not immediately respond to requests for comment on the survivor reactions.
