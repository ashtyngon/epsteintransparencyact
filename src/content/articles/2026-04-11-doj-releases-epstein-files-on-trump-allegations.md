The Department of Justice on April 11 released a batch of previously withheld **Jeffrey Epstein**-related documents that included notes from FBI interviews with a woman who alleged that President **Donald Trump** assaulted her when she was a minor in the 1980s, PBS News reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The documents, which had been held back from earlier releases, contained detailed notes from four separate interviews conducted by FBI agents with the accuser. DOJ officials who reviewed the interviews found the woman credible, according to internal assessment notes included in the release.

## Allegations Span Multiple Years

According to the FBI interview notes, the woman alleged that Epstein brought her to meet Trump on multiple occasions between the ages of 13 and 15. The encounters allegedly took place at properties in New York during the mid-to-late 1980s.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The documents indicated that FBI agents interviewed the woman four times over a period of several months. Each interview produced consistent accounts, and the internal notes reflected that investigators did not identify significant discrepancies in her testimony.

NPR reported that the documents had been withheld from previous releases because DOJ reviewers had classified them as duplicative of materials already published.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> That classification was later determined to be incorrect, and the documents were added to the April 11 release batch.

The White House did not immediately respond to requests for comment on the released documents. Trump has previously denied any wrongdoing related to Epstein and has characterized the transparency act as evidence of his commitment to full disclosure.

## Missing Pages Identified

Reporter **Roger Sollenberger**, who has closely tracked the Epstein document releases, identified 37 pages that appeared to be missing from the batch. Sollenberger reported that the missing pages should have been included based on the DOJ's own indexing system but were absent from the published materials without explanation.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The DOJ did not address the discrepancy in its public statement accompanying the release. A department spokesperson said the agency was committed to full compliance with the Epstein Files Transparency Act and would continue to review materials for publication.

Lawmakers who have been monitoring the releases seized on the missing pages as evidence of ongoing problems with the DOJ's compliance. Members of the House Oversight Committee from both parties called on Acting Attorney General **Todd Blanche** to explain the gaps.

## Legal and Political Implications

Legal experts cautioned that the FBI interview notes, while significant, do not constitute proof of the allegations described. The documents reflect one individual's account as recorded by federal investigators, and no charges were ever filed based on the interviews.

The release nonetheless intensified the political firestorm surrounding the Epstein files. Congressional Democrats called for immediate hearings focused specifically on the newly released materials, while Republican allies of the president dismissed the documents as unsubstantiated allegations from decades ago.

Survivors' advocacy groups said the release underscored the importance of the transparency act and the need for continued scrutiny of the DOJ's review process. Several groups noted that the fact these documents were initially withheld under an incorrect classification raised questions about what other materials might have been similarly miscategorized.
