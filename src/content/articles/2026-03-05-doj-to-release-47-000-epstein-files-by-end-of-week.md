The Department of Justice will release more than 47,000 additional files related to Jeffrey Epstein by the end of this week, the New York Post reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The New York Post did not specify the nature of the documents or which individuals or entities they concern.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Transparency Act Background

The disclosure follows the January 2025 enactment of the [Epstein Transparency Act](/news/epstein-transparency-act-signed-into-law), which mandated federal agencies to declassify and publicly release records related to Epstein. The law passed the House 382-42 in December 2024 and the Senate by unanimous consent days later.

The FBI began its formal review of Epstein-related records in February 2025, as required under the law. The statute created a review board modeled on the John F. Kennedy Assassination Records Collection Act, tasked with collecting and releasing documents from federal agencies.

The Epstein Transparency Act requires federal agencies to organize and release all records related to Epstein, with limited exceptions for national security and privacy concerns.

## Case Background

Jeffrey Epstein was a financier convicted in 2008 on state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida. He died in federal custody in Manhattan in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges.

Federal prosecutors in Manhattan charged Epstein in July 2019 with sex trafficking of minors and conspiracy. The indictment alleged he sexually exploited and abused dozens of underage girls at his residences in Manhattan and Palm Beach, Florida, between 2002 and 2005.

His longtime associate Ghislaine Maxwell was convicted in December 2021 of sex trafficking and conspiracy charges and is currently serving a 20-year federal prison sentence.

The Wall Street Journal reported in March 2026 that the Department of Justice examined several Epstein associates for potential sex crimes over a multiyear period but [charged none of them](/news/2026-03-01-wsj-doj-examined-epstein-associates-for-sex-crimes-charged-none).

## Congressional Inquiries

The House Oversight Committee issued subpoenas in early March 2026 to Bill Gates and Kathryn Ruemmler, a former White House counsel who later worked for Goldman Sachs, as part of its investigation into Epstein's connections with prominent figures.

In January 2024, a federal court in Manhattan unsealed court filings that named dozens of individuals associated with Epstein, though most were previously known from earlier litigation.
