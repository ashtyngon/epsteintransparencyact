The so-called "Epstein curse" is threatening to end Prime Minister **Keir Starmer**'s premiership, according to the Australian Financial Review, as the fallout from **Peter Mandelson**'s connections to **Jeffrey Epstein** continues to engulf the British government.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The AFR report, published April 17, describes a prime minister increasingly isolated by a scandal that has claimed senior advisers, fractured his party, and given opposition leaders ammunition to challenge his credibility in Parliament.

## The Mandelson Vetting Scandal

At the center of the crisis is the revelation that Mandelson, a close Starmer ally and former Labour cabinet minister, failed a security vetting process related to his Epstein connections. Rather than blocking Mandelson's appointment as a senior government adviser, the Foreign Office overruled the vetting outcome and allowed him to proceed, the AFR reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The decision to override the vetting has become a focal point for opposition attacks. Conservative and Liberal Democrat leaders have accused Starmer of misleading Parliament about what he knew regarding Mandelson's failed vetting and when he learned about it.

Mandelson was arrested by UK police earlier this year in connection with allegations that emerged from the Epstein files released under the American transparency act. His arrest marked the highest-profile criminal proceeding directly tied to the documents outside the United States.

## Staff Departures Weaken Starmer

The political damage has been compounded by the departure of key members of Starmer's inner circle. Chief of staff **Morgan McSweeney** and communications director **Tim Allan** both left their positions amid the Epstein-related fallout, leaving the prime minister without two of his most trusted advisers during the deepest crisis of his tenure.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The departures have created a leadership vacuum at the heart of government, according to the AFR, with remaining staff uncertain about the administration's strategy for weathering the scandal. Al Jazeera reported that the staff exits were driven by disagreements over how aggressively to distance the government from Mandelson.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

## Opposition Circles

Scottish Labour leader **Anas Sarwar** previously urged Starmer to resign, a remarkable intervention from within his own party. The call reflected growing unease among Labour figures that the Epstein association was becoming an existential threat to the party's electoral prospects.

Conservative leader **Kemi Badenoch** has used the Mandelson scandal to frame a broader argument about Labour's judgment and trustworthiness, while Liberal Democrat leader **Ed Davey** has called for a full independent inquiry into the vetting override.

The AFR analysis suggested that Starmer's position had become untenable not because of any single revelation but because of the cumulative weight of the Mandelson connection, the staff departures, and the perception that the prime minister had not been forthcoming with Parliament or the public.

UK political analysts quoted in the report gave Starmer's premiership a low probability of surviving through the end of the year, though they noted that the absence of an obvious successor within Labour could prolong his hold on power despite the mounting pressure.

The situation has been closely watched in Washington, where it has been cited by lawmakers pushing for more aggressive use of the Epstein files to hold powerful individuals accountable.
