Rep. **Debbie Wasserman Schultz** (D-FL) introduced legislation on March 10 that would impose visa bans and asset-blocking sanctions on foreign nationals implicated in the **Jeffrey Epstein** files, according to CBS12.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The bill, titled the No Escaping Justice Act, targets individuals outside the United States who have been identified in Epstein-related documents as participants in or facilitators of his sex trafficking operation but who remain beyond the reach of U.S. criminal prosecution.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Visa Bans and Asset Freezes

Under the proposed legislation, the State Department would be required to deny visas to foreign nationals who are implicated in the Epstein files as having engaged in trafficking, abuse, or the facilitation of Epstein's criminal enterprise.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The Treasury Department would simultaneously be directed to block any assets held by those individuals within U.S. jurisdiction.

The sanctions framework mirrors existing mechanisms used to target human rights abusers under the Global Magnitsky Act, which allows the U.S. government to impose financial penalties and travel restrictions on individuals involved in serious human rights violations worldwide.

Wasserman Schultz said the bill was necessary because many of Epstein's associates hold foreign citizenship or reside outside the United States, placing them beyond the practical reach of federal prosecutors. "Justice should not stop at the border," Wasserman Schultz said in a statement from her office.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Presidential Exemption With Accountability

The bill includes a provision allowing the President to exempt specific individuals from the sanctions if doing so is deemed necessary for national security purposes.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> However, any such exemption would trigger a mandatory reporting requirement: the President would be required to provide a written explanation to Congress detailing the reasons for the exemption.

The accountability mechanism is designed to prevent the executive branch from quietly shielding politically connected individuals from the law's consequences. Wasserman Schultz said the provision ensures that any exemption would be subject to congressional scrutiny and public debate.

"If the President decides that protecting a foreign Epstein associate is more important than justice for survivors, the American people deserve to know why," Wasserman Schultz said.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Committee Referral

The No Escaping Justice Act was referred to both the House Foreign Affairs Committee and the House Judiciary Committee, reflecting the bill's dual focus on international sanctions and domestic legal accountability.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The dual referral means the bill must clear both committees before reaching the full House for a vote.

The legislation arrives amid broader congressional efforts to strengthen accountability mechanisms related to the Epstein case. The Epstein Files Transparency Act, signed into law earlier this year, required the release of government documents but did not include provisions for sanctioning individuals named in those documents.

Survivor advocacy groups welcomed the bill as a necessary complement to the transparency act. Organizations representing Epstein's victims have long argued that document releases alone are insufficient without enforcement mechanisms to hold implicated individuals accountable.

The bill's prospects in the Republican-controlled House remain uncertain. While Epstein-related legislation has drawn bipartisan support in recent months, the sanctions framework may face opposition from members who are wary of expanding executive sanctions authority or who have concerns about due process for individuals who have not been criminally charged.

Wasserman Schultz said she is seeking Republican co-sponsors for the bill and is optimistic that the bipartisan momentum behind Epstein accountability measures will carry over to this legislation.
