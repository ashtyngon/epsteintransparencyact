Universities across the United States are facing mounting pressure to reconsider relationships with donors connected to **Jeffrey Epstein** as the ongoing release of documents under the Epstein Files Transparency Act reveals the extent of the convicted sex offender's financial and social network.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Harvard, Ohio State, UCLA, Tufts, and Stony Brook are among the institutions where buildings named for Epstein-linked donors have become flashpoints for student activism and faculty demands for institutional accountability, the Christian Science Monitor reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The donors whose names adorn campus buildings and facilities include **Les Wexner**, **Casey Wasserman**, **Glenn Dubin**, and **Howard Lutnick** — all of whom have appeared in Epstein-related files or reporting in recent months.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Harvard and the Wexner Building

At Harvard's Kennedy School of Government, students and faculty submitted a formal request to rename the Leslie H. Wexner Building and the Wexner-Sunshine Lobby.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup> Wexner, the billionaire founder of L Brands, was designated as an Epstein co-conspirator in a 2019 FBI document released under the transparency act.

The renaming request, which followed months of campus organizing, cited the FBI's co-conspirator designation as grounds for removing Wexner's name from university facilities. Harvard's administration has not publicly committed to a timeline for reviewing the petition.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

WBUR reported that the petition gathered support from faculty members across multiple departments, reflecting broad concern within the university community about the institution's historical relationship with Wexner and, by extension, with Epstein.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

## Ohio State's Review Process

Ohio State University initiated a five-step review process for multiple buildings bearing the Wexner name, including the Les Wexner Football Complex, the Wexner Medical Center, and other campus facilities.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

University President **Ravi Bellamkonda** is overseeing the process, which Fortune reported includes community input, a committee review, and a final recommendation to the Board of Trustees.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup> Ohio State has a particularly extensive naming relationship with Wexner, who donated hundreds of millions of dollars to the university over several decades.

The review represents a significant institutional step, given that renaming facilities can involve complex legal and financial considerations, particularly when naming rights were secured through binding donation agreements.

## Broader Institutional Reckoning

The Hill reported that the donor scrutiny extends beyond Wexner to other prominent figures whose Epstein connections have been documented in released files.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> At UCLA, the naming of facilities connected to donors who appeared in the files has prompted student government resolutions calling for transparency in the university's gift acceptance process.

The Christian Science Monitor noted that the pressure on universities reflects a broader societal reckoning with institutions that accepted money from individuals in Epstein's orbit, often with knowledge of his criminal history or at minimum after his 2008 conviction.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Several universities have responded by establishing or strengthening donor vetting committees and revising gift acceptance policies. However, critics argue that these measures address future donations without resolving the question of existing naming honors for donors now linked to Epstein.

## Legal and Financial Complications

Renaming buildings is not a straightforward process at most universities. Donation agreements frequently include contractual naming provisions that can extend for decades or in perpetuity. Removing a donor's name can trigger legal disputes and, in some cases, require the return of gift funds.

Higher education legal experts have noted that the Epstein-related designations — particularly the FBI's co-conspirator label applied to Wexner — could provide universities with stronger grounds to invoke "moral turpitude" or reputational harm clauses that some naming agreements include.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

None of the named donors have been criminally charged in connection with Epstein's activities.
