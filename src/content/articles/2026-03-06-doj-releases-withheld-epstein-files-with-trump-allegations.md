The Department of Justice released previously withheld files from its Epstein investigation on Friday, including an interview with a woman who made unsubstantiated allegations against President Donald Trump.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The documents had been mistakenly excluded from an earlier disclosure, according to the department.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The release comes as congressional Democrats intensify calls for independent investigation into Trump's ties to Jeffrey Epstein. Rep. Ted Lieu (D-Calif.) called for the appointment of a special counsel to examine claims involving the president.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The newly disclosed files contain an interview with a woman who accused Trump of sexual assault in connection with Epstein.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup> CBS News reported the department characterized the woman's claims as "uncorroborated."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Congressional Response and Oversight Demands

Rep. Nancy Mace (R-S.C.) referenced the Epstein files controversy in remarks directed at the president regarding military action against Iran. "Iran war 'won't make Epstein files go away,'" Mace told Trump, according to The London Economic.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

The statement reflects growing pressure from members of both parties for continued disclosure of documents related to Epstein's criminal network. The Epstein Transparency Act, which Congress passed and Trump signed into law earlier this year, mandates the release of government records related to the deceased financier's sex trafficking operation.

The DOJ's acknowledgment that files were "mistakenly withheld" has raised questions among lawmakers about the completeness of previous document productions under the transparency law.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The department did not specify how many additional pages were included in Friday's release or provide a timeline for when the error was discovered.

## Special Counsel Demand

Lieu's call for a special counsel represents the most direct demand yet from congressional Democrats for an independent prosecutorial review of allegations involving Trump and Epstein.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The California congressman serves on the House Judiciary Committee, which has oversight jurisdiction over the Justice Department.

A special counsel would operate with greater independence from political leadership at the Justice Department, similar to the structure used in previous high-profile investigations involving presidents. Such an appointment would require approval from the attorney general.

The timing of Lieu's statement—following the release of the previously withheld interview—suggests Democrats view the new documents as warranting heightened scrutiny beyond standard DOJ review processes.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

## Context of Document Releases

The Justice Department has released tens of thousands of pages of Epstein-related files in recent weeks under the requirements of the Epstein Transparency Act. Previous disclosures included FBI investigative records, witness interviews, and communications involving Epstein and his associates.

The department initially faced criticism from members of Congress when reports emerged that certain categories of documents had been removed from planned releases. The DOJ later confirmed those exclusions and attributed them to ongoing review processes.

Friday's release marks the first time the department has publicly acknowledged that files were "mistakenly" excluded rather than deliberately withheld for legal reasons.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The distinction could affect congressional oversight efforts, as unintentional omissions may indicate systemic problems with the department's document review protocols.

The woman whose interview appears in the newly released files has made allegations that CBS News reported remain unproven.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The Independent described her as a "Trump sexual assault accuser" in its coverage of the document release.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

Rep. Mace's comment linking the Epstein files to potential military action in Iran suggests some Republicans view the document releases as politically consequential for the Trump administration.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup> The South Carolina congresswoman has previously called for transparency regarding Epstein's associates across party lines.

The convergence of the file releases with foreign policy decisions has created an unusual political dynamic, with members of the president's own party publicly suggesting the administration cannot escape accountability on the Epstein matter through other policy actions.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

Congressional oversight committees have scheduled multiple hearings this month related to Epstein document disclosures. The House Oversight Committee has issued subpoenas to several individuals named in previously released files, including former government officials and business executives who had contact with Epstein.

The Justice Department has not announced any new criminal investigations stemming from the document releases. Previous reporting by The Wall Street Journal found that federal prosecutors examined multiple Epstein associates for potential sex crimes but charged none.

## Ongoing Disclosure Process

The Epstein Transparency Act requires the disclosure of all government records related to Epstein's criminal activities, subject to limited exceptions for national security, ongoing investigations, and privacy protections for victims. The law established a review board to adjudicate disputes over document releases.

The Justice Department has primary responsibility for producing records from the FBI's investigations of Epstein and his network. Other agencies, including the Department of State and the Bureau of Prisons, are also required to release relevant documents under the law.

Friday's acknowledgment of mistaken withholding suggests the review process remains incomplete despite multiple large-scale document productions in recent months.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Congressional oversight committees have requested detailed accounting from the department regarding its document review protocols and quality control measures.

The release of the interview with Trump's accuser represents the most politically sensitive disclosure to date from the DOJ's Epstein files.<sup>[1](#ref-3)</sup> The department's characterization of the claims as uncorroborated may not prevent further congressional inquiry, particularly given Lieu's call for independent investigation.<sup>[1](#ref-2)</sup>
