Former Attorney General **Pam Bondi** is facing the prospect of contempt of Congress proceedings after she failed to appear for a scheduled deposition before the House Oversight Committee on April 14, escalating a bipartisan standoff over her handling of the Jeffrey Epstein files during her tenure at the Department of Justice.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Bondi was fired by President **Donald Trump** on April 2, reportedly over disagreements about how the DOJ managed the release and redaction of documents related to **Jeffrey Epstein**.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Less than two weeks later, she did not show up for sworn testimony that lawmakers on both sides of the aisle had demanded.

## Subpoena Dispute

The House Oversight Committee had subpoenaed Bondi in early March, with five Republican members joining Democrats in the vote to compel her appearance.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The deposition was set for April 14, but Bondi did not appear as scheduled.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

The Department of Justice argued that the subpoena no longer applied to Bondi because she had been terminated and was no longer leading the agency.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> That legal argument was immediately rejected by lawmakers from both parties.

**Rep. Robert Garcia** (D-Calif.), the ranking member of the House Oversight Committee, issued a statement blasting Bondi's failure to appear. Garcia called the no-show an affront to congressional authority and to the public's right to understand how the Epstein files were handled at the highest levels of the Justice Department.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

**Rep. Nancy Mace** (R-S.C.) pushed back on the DOJ's reasoning directly. Mace said the subpoena was issued to Bondi as an individual, not in her capacity as attorney general, and demanded that the deposition be rescheduled immediately.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Mace's position aligned her with Democrats on the committee who have argued that Bondi's firing does not relieve her of the obligation to testify about actions she took while in office.

## Contempt Proceedings

**Rep. Jasmine Crockett** (D-Texas) announced that Democrats would move to hold Bondi in contempt of Congress for defying the subpoena.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup> A contempt referral, if approved by the full committee, would represent a significant escalation and could ultimately be referred to the DOJ for potential criminal prosecution, though enforcement of congressional contempt has historically been uneven.

The bipartisan nature of the original subpoena vote could bolster the case for contempt. Five Republicans broke with party leadership to support compelling Bondi's testimony when the subpoena was first issued in March, reflecting broad frustration with the pace and transparency of the Epstein document releases.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Bondi served as attorney general during the period when the Epstein Files Transparency Act was signed into law, legislation that mandated the disclosure of federal records related to Epstein's criminal activities and the broader network of individuals connected to his operations.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup> She faced bipartisan criticism throughout her tenure for how the DOJ handled redactions in those files, with lawmakers accusing the department of shielding information that Congress and the public had a right to see.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The question now is whether the committee can enforce the subpoena against a private citizen who is no longer serving in government. Legal experts have noted that congressional subpoena power extends to former officials and private individuals, not only to sitting government employees. The DOJ's argument that Bondi's termination nullifies the subpoena has no clear precedent in its favor, according to several constitutional law scholars who have weighed in on the matter.<sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

Neither Bondi nor her legal representatives have issued a public statement explaining her decision not to appear on April 14.
