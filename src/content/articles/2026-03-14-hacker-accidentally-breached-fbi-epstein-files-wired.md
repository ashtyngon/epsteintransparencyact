A hacker who physically broke into an FBI field office inadvertently accessed files related to the Jeffrey Epstein case, according to WIRED's security news roundup.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The breach occurred when an intruder gained unauthorized physical access to FBI facilities, according to the report.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The hacker's actions were not specifically targeting Epstein-related materials, but the files were among those accessed during the intrusion.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The incident represents an unusual security breach involving physical access to FBI facilities rather than a remote cyber intrusion.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> WIRED did not disclose the extent of files accessed, when the breach occurred, or what specific information about Epstein was contained in the compromised files.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Physical vs. Cyber Security Breaches

Physical security breaches at FBI facilities are rare compared to cyber intrusions, making this incident particularly notable given the sensitive nature of Epstein case files.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Physical break-ins require on-site access to buildings, computer terminals, or file storage areas, as opposed to remote network attacks that can be conducted from anywhere in the world.

The FBI has not publicly commented on security measures implemented following the intrusion or whether any criminal charges have been filed against the intruder.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Ongoing FBI Review of Epstein Records

The breach comes as the FBI is conducting a broader review of Epstein-related records following passage of the Epstein Transparency Act, which President Donald Trump signed into law in January 2025.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> 

The law requires declassification and public release of federal government documents related to Epstein and his associate Ghislaine Maxwell within 180 days, with limited exceptions for national security and ongoing law enforcement operations.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The FBI [began its formal review process](/news/fbi-begins-epstein-records-review) to identify documents for release under the new law.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> That review is separate from the security breach reported by WIRED.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Background on Epstein Case

Jeffrey Epstein was a financier who died in federal custody in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> He had previously been convicted in 2008 of state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida and served 13 months in a work-release program.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

Ghislaine Maxwell, Epstein's longtime associate, was convicted in December 2021 of federal sex trafficking charges and is currently serving a 20-year prison sentence.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The Epstein case has generated sustained public interest due to his connections with prominent figures in business, politics, and academia before his death.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> The FBI has maintained custody of extensive files related to the case, including documents that may be subject to public release under the Transparency Act.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The 180-day deadline for document release under the Transparency Act would fall in July 2025, though the law allows for redactions to protect national security interests, ongoing investigations, and privacy of individuals not accused of crimes.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>
