Investigators have opened an inquiry into Jeffrey Epstein's Zorro Ranch in New Mexico, but the probe may face significant obstacles due to the passage of time, The Guardian reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The newspaper reported that authorities are now examining the remote property where Epstein allegedly committed abuses, years after the disgraced financier's death in federal custody in August 2019.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The Guardian characterized the timing as investigators "finally" looking into the ranch, with the headline stating they "may be too late."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## New Mexico Property Under Scrutiny

The sprawling ranch property in New Mexico has long been identified as one of several locations where Epstein allegedly brought victims.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Multiple victims have previously described being transported to various Epstein properties, including locations in New York, Florida, the U.S. Virgin Islands, and New Mexico.

[New Mexico authorities previously launched a full investigation](https://epsteintransparencyact.com/news/2026-02-17-new-mexico-launches-first-full-investigation-into-epstein-s-zorro-ranch) into the Zorro Ranch in February 2026. The state attorney general also [opened a criminal probe into claims of bodies buried at the ranch](https://epsteintransparencyact.com/news/2026-02-19-new-mexico-ag-opens-criminal-probe-into-bodies-buried-at-epstein-ranch) later that month.

The property was one of several Epstein owned across multiple states and countries. Epstein was convicted in 2008 on state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida. He was arrested again in July 2019 on federal sex trafficking charges before his death in custody the following month.

## Timeline Complications

The years-long gap between Epstein's activities at the ranch and the current investigation presents challenges for evidence collection and witness testimony.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Epstein purchased the Zorro Ranch property in the 1990s and allegedly used it for decades before his 2019 arrest.

Federal prosecutors in the Southern District of New York brought sex trafficking charges against Epstein in July 2019, alleging he maintained a network to recruit and abuse dozens of minor girls between 2002 and 2005. Those charges were never resolved due to his death in custody on August 10, 2019.

The ranch property in New Mexico has been mentioned in civil litigation and victim accounts as one of the sites where alleged abuse occurred. The property was later sold following Epstein's death as part of the settlement of his estate.

## Broader Investigation Context

The FBI began a broader review of Epstein-related records under the Epstein Transparency Act, which was signed into law requiring declassification of documents related to the case. The law mandated the release of FBI files related to Epstein's activities and associates.

Epstein's associate Ghislaine Maxwell was convicted in December 2021 on federal charges of sex trafficking minors and conspiracy. She is currently serving a 20-year prison sentence. Maxwell's trial included testimony about activities at multiple Epstein properties, though the New Mexico ranch featured less prominently than other locations in that prosecution.

The Department of Justice released millions of pages of Epstein-related documents in February 2026 following passage of the transparency law. Those files included correspondence, financial records, and investigative materials spanning decades of FBI scrutiny of Epstein's network.
