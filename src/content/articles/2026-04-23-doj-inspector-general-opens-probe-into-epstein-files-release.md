The U.S. **Department of Justice's Office of the Inspector General** announced on April 23, 2026 that it has opened an investigation into how the DOJ has handled the release of files related to **Jeffrey Epstein**, focusing on the agency's compliance with the Epstein Files Transparency Act.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The probe will examine "the DOJ's identification, collection, and production of responsive material," along with the department's "processes for redacting and withholding material" and how it has addressed problems that emerged after Epstein documents were released, according to reporting by CNN, NBC News, and the Washington Post.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup><sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup><sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

## Scope of the Inspector General Investigation

The investigation comes amid sustained complaints from lawmakers in both parties that the Justice Department has failed to comply with the Transparency Act, which was signed into law earlier this year and required the DOJ to disclose federal investigative records related to **Jeffrey Epstein** and **Ghislaine Maxwell**.

The DOJ missed the law's 30-day statutory deadline for full disclosure, according to multiple outlets covering the inspector general announcement.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup><sup>[4](#ref-4)</sup>

The OIG will also review how the department handled redactions in light of revelations that personal information for approximately 100 Epstein victims had been published in earlier file releases. That privacy failure is now the subject of a separate class action lawsuit filed in March against the DOJ and Google.

The watchdog will produce a public report on its findings, although officials have not announced a timeline for completion.<sup>[5](#ref-5)</sup>

## Background and Political Pressure

The investigation lands in the middle of a turbulent period for the Justice Department's handling of the Epstein matter. **President Donald Trump** fired Attorney General **Pam Bondi** on April 2, in a move multiple outlets reported was tied to dissatisfaction with her management of the file releases. Deputy Attorney General **Todd Blanche** has been serving as acting attorney general since then.

Lawmakers from both parties have accused the Trump administration of using heavy redactions to shield politically connected figures named in the files. House Oversight Committee Democrats have separately moved to hold Bondi in contempt of Congress for refusing to appear at an April 14 deposition about the file releases.

The Inspector General's review is independent of the political fights playing out in Congress and the DOJ. The OIG operates as the Justice Department's internal watchdog and reports findings to both the attorney general and Congress.

## What Comes Next

The OIG announcement does not place any direct restrictions on the DOJ's ongoing handling of the Epstein files. It does, however, formalize a fact-finding process that lawmakers, survivors, and journalists have demanded for months as discrepancies have emerged between what the DOJ claimed to release and what congressional reviewers said they could find.

A public OIG report could provide the first independent accounting of how the department made decisions about which Epstein-related materials to release, redact, or withhold under the Transparency Act.
