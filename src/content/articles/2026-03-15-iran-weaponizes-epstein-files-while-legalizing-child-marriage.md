Iranian state media has begun promoting newly released U.S. government documents related to Jeffrey Epstein as evidence of Western moral corruption, even as the Islamic Republic maintains laws permitting marriage of girls as young as 13, according to WION.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

WION characterized Iran's approach as "propaganda of hypocrisy," pointing to the contrast between the country's criticism of Western officials' associations with Epstein and its own legal framework regarding minors.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The outlet reported that Iran is "weaponising" the Epstein files for political purposes.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Under Iranian law, girls can be legally married at age 13, WION reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The outlet noted this legal reality while documenting Iran's state media focus on the recently released FBI and Justice Department documents related to Epstein's network of associates.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The FBI and Justice Department have released multiple batches of Epstein-related documents in recent weeks under the [Epstein Transparency Act](/news/epstein-transparency-act-signed-into-law), which was signed into law earlier this year.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The releases have included photographs, flight logs, and investigative materials related to Epstein's connections with prominent individuals across business, politics, and academia.

## International Response to Document Releases

Media outlets worldwide have reported on the contents of newly public materials. Iranian state media's focus on the documents represents part of a broader pattern of international attention to the file releases, according to WION.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The Epstein Transparency Act requires federal agencies to review and release documents related to Epstein's crimes and associations, with certain exceptions for national security and ongoing investigations.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Jeffrey Epstein died in federal custody in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges. His associate, Ghislaine Maxwell, was convicted in 2021 on federal charges of sex trafficking minors and is currently serving a 20-year prison sentence.

WION's reporting highlighted what it described as a disconnect between Iran's international messaging around the Epstein files and the country's domestic policies on child marriage.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The outlet suggested Iran's state media campaigns serve primarily as tools for geopolitical messaging.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Additional document releases are expected in coming months as federal agencies continue their review processes under the transparency law's requirements.
