Bank of America has reached a settlement in principle with victims of Jeffrey Epstein, according to News24.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The settlement resolves litigation in which victims alleged the bank facilitated Epstein's sex trafficking operations by maintaining his accounts and processing related transactions. A settlement in principle indicates the parties have agreed to major terms but have not finalized all details or executed formal documents.

The financial terms and number of victims included in the agreement were not disclosed.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Banking Litigation Against Financial Institutions

Bank of America is the third major financial institution to settle claims from Epstein victims over its banking relationship with the convicted sex offender.

JPMorgan Chase agreed to pay $290 million in June 2023 to settle a class-action lawsuit brought by Epstein victims. That settlement covered claims that the bank ignored red flags about Epstein's trafficking activities while he was a client from 1998 to 2013.

Deutsche Bank reached a $75 million settlement with Epstein victims in May 2023. The German bank maintained Epstein as a client after JPMorgan severed ties with him in 2013.

A federal judge [previously allowed the Bank of America lawsuit to proceed](/news/2026-02-13-court-allows-bofa-epstein-trafficking-lawsuit-to-proceed), finding that victims had presented sufficient evidence that the bank may have knowingly benefited from Epstein's trafficking operation.

## Claims Against Banks

The lawsuits against these financial institutions centered on allegations that banks violated federal anti-trafficking laws and failed to file required suspicious activity reports despite evidence of Epstein's crimes.

Victims argued the banks processed payments to women who were recruited into Epstein's trafficking network, facilitated cash withdrawals that funded his operations, and maintained accounts despite knowledge of his 2008 conviction.

The litigation sought to establish that financial institutions can be held liable under federal law when they provide banking services that enable sex trafficking, even if they are not directly involved in the trafficking itself.

## Epstein Case Background

Epstein pleaded guilty in 2008 to Florida state charges of soliciting a minor under a controversial plea agreement that allowed him to avoid federal prosecution at that time. He was arrested again in July 2019 on federal sex trafficking charges and died in federal custody in August 2019 while awaiting trial.

His longtime associate Ghislaine Maxwell was convicted in December 2021 on federal charges of sex trafficking and conspiracy for her role in recruiting and grooming underage girls for Epstein. She is serving a 20-year prison sentence.

The banking settlements represent one of several avenues through which Epstein's victims have sought accountability from institutions and individuals they allege enabled his trafficking operation over multiple decades.
