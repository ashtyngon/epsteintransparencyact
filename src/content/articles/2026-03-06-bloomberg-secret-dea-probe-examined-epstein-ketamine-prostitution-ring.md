The Drug Enforcement Administration conducted a previously undisclosed investigation into **Jeffrey Epstein** that examined his alleged involvement with ketamine distribution, money transfers, and sex trafficking, according to Bloomberg.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The probe, which has not been previously reported, adds to the growing list of federal investigations into Epstein's activities before his August 2019 death in federal custody.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Bloomberg reported details of the DEA investigation, though the exact timeframe and outcome of the inquiry were not disclosed.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## DEA Investigation Scope

The DEA's investigation reportedly focused on three primary areas: Epstein's alleged connection to ketamine, a controlled substance; money transfers; and activities related to sex trafficking.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> 

Ketamine, a dissociative anesthetic with legitimate medical uses, is classified as a Schedule III controlled substance under federal law. Schedule III drugs are defined as having moderate to low potential for physical and psychological dependence. Distribution of ketamine requires DEA registration, and unauthorized distribution can result in federal criminal charges.

The investigation's focus on money transfers suggests federal authorities were examining potential financial networks related to Epstein's alleged criminal activities.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The DEA has jurisdiction over drug trafficking cases and routinely investigates financial crimes connected to controlled substance distribution, including money laundering schemes that facilitate drug operations.

## Federal Investigation Timeline

The revelation of the DEA probe comes as multiple federal agencies face renewed scrutiny over their handling of Epstein-related investigations. The Wall Street Journal [previously reported](/news/2026-03-01-wsj-doj-examined-epstein-associates-for-sex-crimes-charged-none) that the Department of Justice examined Epstein associates for potential sex crimes but ultimately charged none.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Epstein died by suicide on August 10, 2019, while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges in New York. He had previously been convicted in 2008 on state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida under a controversial non-prosecution agreement that allowed him to avoid federal prosecution at that time. That plea deal, negotiated by then-U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta, permitted Epstein to serve just 13 months in county jail with work release privileges.

The existence of a separate DEA investigation raises questions about the scope of federal law enforcement's knowledge of Epstein's activities and whether information was shared between agencies. Bloomberg's reporting did not indicate whether the DEA investigation resulted in any charges, referrals to other agencies, or enforcement actions.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Congressional Oversight

Congress has intensified oversight of Epstein-related matters following passage of legislation requiring document disclosure. The House Oversight Committee has [issued subpoenas to multiple individuals](/news/2026-03-04-house-panel-subpoenas-bill-gates-kathryn-ruemmler-in-epstein-probe) with alleged connections to Epstein as part of its ongoing investigation.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The [FBI began reviewing Epstein-related records](/news/fbi-begins-epstein-records-review) following passage of the transparency legislation. That review process is ongoing, though the DEA investigation details reported by Bloomberg were not included in previously released document batches.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Federal prosecutors in New York successfully convicted **Ghislaine Maxwell**, Epstein's longtime associate, on sex trafficking charges in December 2021. She was sentenced to 20 years in federal prison in June 2022 and is currently incarcerated. No other individuals have been charged with crimes related to Epstein's sex trafficking operation, despite years of federal investigation by multiple agencies.

The disclosure of the DEA probe adds another dimension to the timeline of federal law enforcement activity related to Epstein, whose connections to prominent figures in business, politics, and academia have been the subject of extensive reporting and congressional scrutiny.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>
