A CNN investigation has determined that approximately 2.5 million pages of **Jeffrey Epstein**-related documents held by the Department of Justice remain unreleased to the public, raising questions about the completeness of the government's transparency efforts.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The network's reporting found that while the DOJ has published roughly 3.5 million pages of Epstein-related material, many of those documents contain heavy redactions that obscure key information. The 2.5 million unreleased pages represent a significant portion of the department's total Epstein holdings.

## Scope of Unreleased Material

CNN reported that the unreleased documents include investigative files compiled by FBI agents, internal DOJ communications about the Epstein case, and evidence gathered during federal probes of Epstein's activities spanning multiple jurisdictions.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The material also covers the department's internal deliberations about prosecution decisions, including the controversial 2008 non-prosecution agreement negotiated by then-U.S. Attorney Alexander Acosta in Florida. That agreement allowed Epstein to plead to state charges and avoid federal prosecution for over a decade.

Legal experts told CNN that these files could contain information relevant to public understanding of the scope of Epstein's network and the government's response to it.

## Redaction Concerns

Beyond the unreleased documents, CNN found that released pages contain extensive redactions that in some cases remove entire paragraphs or pages of content. The department has cited several justifications for redactions, including protection of ongoing investigations, national security, and the privacy of individuals not charged with crimes.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Lawmakers from both parties have questioned whether the redactions are excessive. The Epstein Transparency Act required release of documents with limited exceptions, and congressional critics have argued the DOJ has interpreted those exceptions too broadly.

AG **Pam Bondi** previously told Congress that all releasable Epstein files had been published. CNN's findings directly contradicted that claim by documenting the volume of material the department has retained.

## Congressional Response

Members of the House Oversight Committee cited the CNN investigation in renewed calls for the DOJ to release the remaining documents. Rep. Robert Garcia (D-CA) said the findings confirmed what the committee's investigation had indicated — that the department was withholding a substantial volume of relevant material.

"Two and a half million pages is not a small oversight," Garcia said. "This is a deliberate decision to keep the public in the dark about the full scope of the Epstein matter."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Republican members of the committee, including several who co-authored the Epstein Transparency Act, have also expressed concern about the volume of unreleased material. The bipartisan criticism has strengthened the committee's position in its ongoing disputes with DOJ.

## Legal Action Considered

Lawmakers told CNN they are considering legal action to compel the release of the remaining documents. Options include seeking a court order to enforce the Transparency Act's disclosure requirements or filing additional subpoenas for specific categories of withheld material.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Legal experts said congressional standing to sue has been strengthened by the act, which created a statutory mandate for release.

The DOJ did not respond to CNN's request for comment on the investigation's findings. Department spokespeople have previously said all releases comply with the Transparency Act's provisions.

## Implications for Accountability

The gap between released and held documents has implications for ongoing civil litigation, international investigations, and public understanding of Epstein's network. Several foreign governments have cited U.S. document releases as the basis for their own probes.

Survivor advocates said the unreleased files may contain information important to victims seeking accountability through court proceedings in multiple jurisdictions.
