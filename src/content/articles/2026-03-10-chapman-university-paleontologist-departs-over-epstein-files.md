Paleontologist **John "Jack" Horner**, widely known as the real-life inspiration for the lead character in the Jurassic Park film franchise, has left Chapman University after his name surfaced in **Jeffrey Epstein**-related files released in January, Inside Higher Ed reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Horner, who had held a position at the Orange, California, university, departed following the disclosure that he had visited Epstein's Zorro Ranch in New Mexico in 2012 on what appeared to be a fossil-hunting expedition.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Chapman University has not publicly clarified whether Horner resigned voluntarily or was terminated.

The documents placing Horner at Epstein's sprawling New Mexico property were among thousands of pages released under the Epstein Files Transparency Act. The ranch, located outside Stanley, New Mexico, has been a focus of federal investigation, with the FBI conducting a search of the property in March 2026.

## A Prominent Scientific Figure

Horner built his career as one of the world's most recognized paleontologists. His groundbreaking research on dinosaur growth and behavior at Montana State University, where he spent decades as curator of paleontology at the Museum of the Rockies, made him a leading figure in the field.

Film director Steven Spielberg consulted Horner extensively during the production of the original Jurassic Park in 1993, and the character of Dr. Alan Grant, played by Sam Neill, was widely understood to be based in part on Horner's work and personality. Horner served as a technical adviser on all four films in the original series.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

After retiring from Montana State, Horner joined Chapman University, where he continued research and teaching. His presence at a smaller private university was considered a significant recruitment achievement for the institution's science programs.

## The Ranch Visit

The 2012 visit to Epstein's ranch occurred four years after Epstein's sex offense conviction in Florida. The files indicate the trip was related to fossil collection, an activity consistent with Horner's professional expertise.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Epstein's Zorro Ranch, spanning thousands of acres in a geologically rich area of New Mexico, was known to have hosted various scientists and researchers over the years. Epstein used invitations to his properties as a means of building relationships with prominent academics and researchers across multiple disciplines.

The files did not indicate whether Horner received research funding from Epstein or his affiliated foundations. The nature and extent of any professional or financial relationship beyond the documented ranch visit remains unclear from the released documents.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Institutional Response

Chapman University issued a brief statement acknowledging Horner's departure but did not provide details about the circumstances. The university did not respond to follow-up questions from Inside Higher Ed about whether it had conducted an internal review of Horner's Epstein connections.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The lack of clarity about whether the separation was voluntary or involuntary reflects a pattern at several universities where faculty members named in the Epstein files have quietly departed without public explanation. In some cases, institutions and departing faculty have reached confidential agreements that preclude public disclosure of the terms.

Horner's departure is the latest in a series of academic casualties resulting from the ongoing release of Epstein-related documents. Faculty members at UCLA, UC San Diego, Carnegie Mellon, and other institutions have also faced consequences after their connections to Epstein were documented in the files.

## Broader Academic Reckoning

The exits have intensified debate within higher education about the obligations of universities to vet donors and the personal associations of their faculty. Academic governance bodies at several institutions have called for clearer policies requiring disclosure of relationships with individuals convicted of serious crimes.
