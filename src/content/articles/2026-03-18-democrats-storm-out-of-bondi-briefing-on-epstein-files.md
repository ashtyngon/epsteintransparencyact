Democratic members of the House Oversight Committee walked out of a closed-door briefing with Attorney General **Pam Bondi** and Deputy Attorney General **Todd Blanche** on March 18, escalating a confrontation over the handling of **Jeffrey Epstein**-related documents.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The walkout came after Bondi declined to commit to complying with an April 14 subpoena that would require her to provide sworn testimony before the committee.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The briefing, held behind closed doors, was intended to address growing congressional concerns about the pace and completeness of document releases under the Epstein Files Transparency Act.

## 'Fake Hearing' Accusations

Rep. **Robert Garcia** (D-CA) told reporters after exiting the briefing that the session amounted to a "fake hearing" because Bondi was not placed under oath.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Without the oath requirement, Democrats argued, there was no mechanism to hold the attorney general accountable for the accuracy of her statements.

"We are not going to sit through a briefing where the attorney general can say whatever she wants with no consequences," Garcia said, according to NBC News.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup>

The subpoena for Bondi's sworn testimony was formally issued on March 17 by Chairman **James Comer** (R-KY), following a bipartisan committee vote of 24-19.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup> Five Republican members crossed party lines to join all Democrats in authorizing the subpoena, a rare display of bipartisan agreement on the politically charged issue.

## Subpoena Sets April 14 Deadline

The April 14 deadline gives the Department of Justice roughly four weeks to prepare for Bondi's appearance. The subpoena requires Bondi to testify under oath about the DOJ's review process for Epstein-related files, including decisions about which documents to release, which to redact, and which to withhold entirely.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Comer has maintained that the committee has a legitimate oversight interest in how the executive branch is implementing the transparency law signed by President **Donald Trump** earlier this year. The law requires federal agencies to declassify and release documents related to Epstein's activities and associates.

Democrats on the committee have accused the DOJ of slow-walking releases and selectively redacting documents to protect politically connected individuals.<sup>[2](#ref-2)</sup> Republicans who supported the subpoena have expressed similar frustration with the pace of compliance.

The DOJ has not publicly commented on whether Bondi will comply with the April 14 subpoena. Legal experts have noted that disputes between Congress and the executive branch over subpoena compliance can take months or years to resolve in court if the administration chooses to resist.<sup>[3](#ref-3)</sup>

The confrontation represents one of the most direct clashes between Congress and the Trump administration over the Epstein files since the transparency act became law. Both parties have faced pressure from advocacy groups and survivors to ensure the full scope of Epstein's network is made public.
