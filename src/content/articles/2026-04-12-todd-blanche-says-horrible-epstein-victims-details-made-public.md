Acting Attorney General **Todd Blanche** said April 12 that he was "horrified" that identifying details of **Jeffrey Epstein**'s victims had been included in documents released by the Department of Justice, calling the exposure "horrible" for the individuals affected.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Blanche's comments, made during a press conference at DOJ headquarters, came as approximately 100 survivors pursue a class action lawsuit alleging that the department's document releases violated their privacy by including names, addresses, and other identifying information that should have been redacted.

## Class Action Lawsuit Grows

The lawsuit, filed in federal court, alleges that the DOJ failed to implement adequate redaction protocols before publishing millions of pages of Epstein-related documents under the transparency act. The plaintiffs include survivors whose identities were previously protected by court orders and sealed agreements.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Attorneys representing the survivors said the number of plaintiffs had grown steadily since the initial filing and could increase further as more individuals discovered that their personal information appeared in the released materials.

"The DOJ's carelessness has undone all of that protection."

Blanche acknowledged that the redaction process had failed in a number of cases and said the department was conducting an internal review to determine how the errors occurred. He did not commit to a specific timeline for completing the review.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Blanche Inherits Troubled Process

Blanche assumed leadership of the DOJ on April 2 after President **Donald Trump** fired former Attorney General **Pam Bondi** over her handling of the Epstein file releases. The redaction failures were among the issues that contributed to bipartisan frustration with Bondi's tenure.

The acting attorney general said he had ordered a comprehensive audit of all documents that had been released to identify additional instances where victim information may have been improperly disclosed. He also directed DOJ staff to implement enhanced review procedures for future releases.

However, Blanche stopped short of announcing any pause in the release schedule. Lawmakers from both parties have pressured the DOJ to continue publishing documents without delay, and any suspension could be seen as backtracking on the administration's transparency commitments.

## Tension Between Transparency and Privacy

The incident has sharpened the tension between the public demand for full disclosure of Epstein-related records and the legal and ethical obligation to protect victims' identities. Survivors' advocates have argued that both goals can be achieved simultaneously if the DOJ commits adequate resources to the redaction process.

Several members of Congress said Blanche's acknowledgment of the problem was a necessary first step but called for concrete action. Rep. **Nancy Mace** (R-SC) said the DOJ should immediately hire additional staff dedicated solely to reviewing documents for victim-identifying information before any further batches are released.

The class action lawsuit seeks both monetary damages and injunctive relief requiring the DOJ to remove improperly disclosed information from its public databases and implement court-supervised redaction procedures going forward.

Legal experts noted that the case raised novel questions about government liability for privacy violations committed in the course of complying with a congressional mandate. The outcome could set precedent for how future large-scale document disclosures are handled.
