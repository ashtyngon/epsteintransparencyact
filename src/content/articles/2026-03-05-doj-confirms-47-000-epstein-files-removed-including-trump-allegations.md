The Department of Justice confirmed that more than **47,000 files related to Jeffrey Epstein** were removed from federal records, including allegations involving former President **Donald Trump**, according to Tyla.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The confirmation addresses the scope of documents purged from the system, with the DOJ acknowledging the removal in response to inquiries about missing Epstein-related materials.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The files reportedly included allegations concerning Trump's interactions with the disgraced financier.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Scale of Document Removal

The **47,000-file figure** represents a substantial portion of federal records connected to the Epstein case.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The DOJ's confirmation provides the first official accounting of how many documents were taken out of circulation.

The nature and content of the specific Trump allegations in the removed files were not detailed in the DOJ's confirmation.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The removal of files containing these allegations adds to ongoing questions about which materials related to Epstein's network of associates have been preserved and which have been eliminated from official records.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

The DOJ has not publicly disclosed when the files were removed, who authorized their removal, or whether any copies were retained elsewhere in federal systems.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The department's confirmation does not specify whether the removal was part of standard record-retention practices, a deliberate purge, or another administrative process.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Background on Transparency Requirements

Jeffrey Epstein died in federal custody in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges. His case involved decades of alleged abuse of underage girls and connections to numerous powerful figures in business, politics, and academia.

The [Epstein Transparency Act, signed into law in December 2024](/news/epstein-transparency-act-signed-into-law), requires federal agencies to review and declassify documents related to Epstein's activities and associates. The law mandates that agencies disclose records unless they meet specific exemption criteria related to national security or ongoing investigations.

Federal agencies have been conducting reviews of Epstein-related materials under the new transparency requirements. The [FBI began its formal review process](/news/fbi-begins-epstein-records-review) to identify records eligible for public release, though the timeline for document production has extended beyond initial expectations.

Court documents and materials released through civil litigation have provided some insight into Epstein's network, but significant portions of the investigative record remain sealed or inaccessible. The Wall Street Journal reported in March 2026 that the [Department of Justice examined multiple Epstein associates for potential sex crimes but charged none](/news/2026-03-01-wsj-doj-examined-epstein-associates-for-sex-crimes-charged-none).

## Congressional Investigations

Congressional committees have ramped up their examination of Epstein-related matters in recent months. The House Oversight Committee [issued subpoenas to Bill Gates and former Obama White House Counsel Kathryn Ruemmler](/news/2026-03-04-house-panel-subpoenas-bill-gates-kathryn-ruemmler-in-epstein-probe) as part of its probe into Epstein's connections to powerful figures.

Rep. **Ro Khanna (D-Calif.)** stated in February 2026 that he expects [more names to emerge from Epstein files](/news/2026-02-28-rep-ro-khanna-more-names-will-emerge-from-epstein-files) as transparency efforts continue. The revelation of removed files containing Trump allegations could intensify scrutiny of what other materials may have been eliminated from federal records.

The confirmation of the file removal comes amid broader debates about accountability for those connected to Epstein's criminal enterprise and whether federal authorities have fully investigated his network of associates and enablers.
