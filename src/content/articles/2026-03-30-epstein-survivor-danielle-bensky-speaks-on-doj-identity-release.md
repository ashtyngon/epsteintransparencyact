**Danielle Bensky**, a survivor of **Jeffrey Epstein**'s sex trafficking operation, spoke publicly on March 30 about the class action lawsuit filed against the Department of Justice and Google over the release of survivor-identifying information in Epstein-related documents.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

In an interview with NPR, Bensky described how her name appeared in multiple document batches released by the DOJ under the Epstein Files Transparency Act, despite the law's explicit protections requiring the redaction of victim-identifying information.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Repeated Exposure Across Document Batches

Bensky told NPR that her name surfaced not once but multiple times across different sets of documents released on separate dates.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The pattern of repeated exposure has raised questions among survivor advocates about whether the failure to redact victim information was the result of negligence or something more deliberate.

"The first time I saw my name, I thought it was a mistake," Bensky said. "The second time, I started to wonder. By the third time, it was hard to believe this was accidental."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Bensky said she is not a party to the class action lawsuit filed on March 27 by approximately 100 survivors against the DOJ and Google, but she was among those whose identities were exposed through the document releases. She said she chose to speak publicly because the damage to her privacy had already been done.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

## Impact on Survivors

Bensky described the experience of being publicly identified as deeply re-traumatizing. She said she received messages from strangers who found her information through online searches, some expressing sympathy and others making her feel unsafe.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

"You spend years trying to rebuild your life," Bensky told NPR. "And then the government puts your name out there and suddenly everyone knows."<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>

Survivor advocacy organizations have said Bensky's experience is representative of what many of the approximately 100 outed survivors have endured since the document releases began. The organizations have called for an immediate halt to further document releases until the DOJ implements a more rigorous redaction process.

## Legal and Legislative Response

The class action lawsuit filed on behalf of survivors seeks to compel the DOJ to re-review all released documents and to require Google to de-index pages containing survivor-identifying information. Legal experts have said the case could set important precedent for how the government balances transparency mandates with victim protection obligations.

Members of Congress from both parties have expressed concern about the survivor exposure issue. Several lawmakers who supported the Epstein Files Transparency Act have said the law was never intended to expose victims and have called on the DOJ to explain how the redaction failures occurred.

The DOJ has not publicly addressed Bensky's specific claims about her name appearing in multiple document batches. The department has said it is committed to protecting victim identities while complying with the transparency law.

Bensky said she hopes her decision to speak publicly will pressure the government to take the redaction failures seriously. "If they can do this to us, they can do it to anyone," she said.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup>
