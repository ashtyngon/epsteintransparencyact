A foreign hacker breached FBI systems containing Jeffrey Epstein-related records through a physical break-in at a federal facility, according to International Business Times UK.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The incident targeted files connected to the deceased financier's criminal case and associated individuals.

The breach involved direct physical access to FBI systems, International Business Times UK reported.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Federal officials have not publicly disclosed the hacker's nationality, the specific facility targeted, or the scope of information accessed during the intrusion.

The incident occurred as the FBI conducts an [ongoing review of Epstein-related records](/news/fbi-begins-epstein-records-review) under the Jeffrey Epstein Transparency Act, which became law in January 2025.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The law requires federal agencies to declassify and release documents related to Epstein's criminal activities and associations within specified timeframes.

## Background on Epstein Case

Epstein, a wealthy financier, died in August 2019 while awaiting trial on federal sex trafficking charges in a Manhattan jail cell.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The New York City medical examiner ruled his death a suicide. He had previously been convicted in 2008 on state charges of soliciting a minor in Florida under a controversial plea agreement that allowed him to avoid federal prosecution.

The FBI has maintained extensive files on Epstein dating back years before his 2019 arrest.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> These records include investigative materials, witness statements, evidence documentation, and information about individuals connected to his criminal activities. The transparency act requires the release of such materials, subject to redactions for ongoing investigations, national security concerns, and privacy protections.

Federal agencies have released thousands of pages of Epstein-related documents in recent weeks, including FBI investigative files, emails, and financial records.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The releases have identified hundreds of individuals who had contact with Epstein, though contact alone does not indicate wrongdoing or knowledge of criminal activity.

## Physical Access to Federal Systems

Physical breaches of FBI facilities represent significant security failures, as direct access to computer systems allows intruders to bypass digital security measures designed to prevent remote hacking attempts.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Such intrusions can enable the copying of files, installation of monitoring software, or extraction of data from secured networks that are isolated from internet connections.

Federal facilities housing sensitive law enforcement records typically maintain multiple layers of physical security, including access controls, surveillance systems, and restricted areas requiring special authorization.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The FBI has not disclosed how the hacker circumvented these security measures or what systems were compromised during the breach.

Foreign intelligence services routinely target U.S. law enforcement records related to high-profile investigations, particularly those involving individuals with international connections.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> Epstein maintained residences and business interests in multiple countries and had documented associations with prominent figures across several nations.

## Ongoing Document Releases

The [FBI recently searched Epstein's Zorro Ranch](/news/fbi-searches-epstein-s-zorro-ranch-after-new-revelations) property in New Mexico, marking renewed investigative activity years after his death.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> That search followed revelations that prompted federal authorities to reexamine evidence from the sprawling property where Epstein allegedly brought young women.

Multiple lawmakers have questioned the pace of FBI document review and release under the transparency law, though the agency has cited the complexity of redacting sensitive information while complying with disclosure requirements.<sup>[1](#ref-1)</sup> The compromise of investigative files could potentially complicate efforts to protect witness identities, ongoing investigative methods, or information related to individuals who have not been charged with crimes.
