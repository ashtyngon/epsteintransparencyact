On February 19, 2026 — his 66th birthday — Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor was arrested by London's Metropolitan Police on suspicion of misconduct in public office. It was the first arrest of a senior member of the British royal family in roughly four centuries, an event without modern precedent.

## What Triggered It

The arrest followed weeks of new revelations drawn from Epstein-related documents. Roughly two weeks earlier, an anti-monarchy campaign group had called for police to investigate records indicating that Andrew, while serving as a U.K. trade envoy between about 2001 and 2011, had shared confidential government information with Epstein. The material referenced briefings on official trips — to Singapore, China, Hong Kong, and Vietnam in 2010 and 2011 — that Andrew was alleged to have passed to the financier. Misconduct in public office is a common-law offense carrying a potential life sentence.

## Arrested, Not Charged

Andrew was held for about 11 hours and released "under investigation" — meaning he had been neither charged nor cleared. The Metropolitan Police had earlier expanded its inquiry to question his former protection officers about his Epstein connections. In May 2026, the U.K. government released a tranche of files on his trade-envoy years, keeping the matter in the headlines.

## Reverberations

King Charles III expressed his "deepest concern" and called for "the full, fair and proper process." The arrest reframed the British end of the scandal from a question of honors and reputation into one of criminal liability. In Washington, it sharpened a pointed contrast: U.S. lawmakers — noting that no American beyond Epstein and Maxwell had been criminally charged, and that even Leslie Wexner said he had never been contacted by the FBI — asked why the United States had not followed Britain's lead. "Who's next?" became a recurring question on both sides of the Atlantic.
